\begin{abstract}
We address the task of automatic MeSH (Medical Subject Headings) term assignment to biomedical articles,
formulated as a multi-label classification problem. Using only the title and abstract of each article,
we compare a Word2Vec + MLP baseline against PubMedBERT, a transformer pre-trained exclusively on
PubMed abstracts, on a coverage-sampled subset of the BioASQ 2022 allMeSH dataset.
Our results show that PubMedBERT achieves a micro-F1 of 0.476 and a macro-F1 of 0.185 on the test set,
substantially outperforming the Word2Vec + MLP baseline (micro-F1 = 0.340, macro-F1 = 0.039).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Medical literature is indexed in PubMed using MeSH (Medical Subject Headings), a controlled vocabulary
maintained by the National Library of Medicine. Manually assigning MeSH terms to the growing volume of
biomedical publications is time-consuming and expensive. Automatic biomedical indexing, the task of
predicting MeSH terms from an article's text, therefore has significant practical value.

This paper presents our approach to the BioASQ Task A challenge~\cite{bioasq2015}, which frames the
problem as multi-label classification: each article may be assigned multiple MeSH terms simultaneously.
We focus exclusively on the title and abstract as input, and compare a classical word embedding baseline
against a fine-tuned biomedical transformer model.

The rest of this paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.
Section~\ref{sec:method} describes our approach. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents experimental
results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Early approaches to biomedical article indexing relied on TF-IDF weighted bag-of-words representations
combined with k-nearest neighbours (MTI,~\cite{mti}) or SVMs. The introduction of word embeddings,
such as Word2Vec~\cite{mikolov2013} and fastText~\cite{joulin2017}, improved performance by capturing
semantic similarity between medical terms.

More recently, transformer-based models pre-trained on large biomedical corpora have shown strong results.
BioBERT~\cite{lee2020biobert} and SciBERT~\cite{beltagy2019scibert} both extend BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert}
with domain-specific pre-training. PubMedBERT~\cite{pubmedbert} goes further by pre-training from scratch
exclusively on PubMed abstracts, avoiding vocabulary mismatch with general-domain text.
In the BioASQ challenge, fine-tuned transformer models consistently outperform shallow approaches,
with MeSH-CNN~\cite{meshcnn} and AttentionMeSH~\cite{attentionmesh} being notable prior systems.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Data Sampling}
\label{sec:sampling}

The full BioASQ 2022 allMeSH corpus contains 16{,}218{,}838 articles annotated with 29{,}681 unique MeSH
terms. Training on the full corpus is computationally prohibitive, so we construct a representative
100{,}000-article sample using a coverage-first strategy:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Coverage pass}: stream through the full corpus and greedily include any article that
    contains at least one MeSH label observed fewer than 5 times so far. This guarantees full label
    coverage. In practice, 97{,}743 articles are needed to cover all 29{,}681 labels at least 5 times.
    \item \textbf{Fill}: the remaining slots (up to the target size) are drawn using uniform reservoir
    sampling over articles whose labels are already sufficiently covered.
\end{enumerate}

MeSH labels appearing fewer than 10 times in the final sample are discarded at training time,
yielding a vocabulary of 13{,}898 labels for the 100K sample.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given the title $t$ and abstract $a$ of a biomedical article, the goal is to predict a subset
$\hat{Y} \subseteq L$ of MeSH labels from the full label set $L$.
This is a multi-label classification problem with $|L|$ binary outputs.

\subsection{Baseline: Word2Vec + MLP}

As a baseline we train a 200-dimensional Word2Vec model~\cite{mikolov2013} on the sampled corpus using
a window size of 5 and 5 training epochs. Each article is represented as the mean of its token vectors.
A single-hidden-layer MLP (512 units, ReLU) is then trained on the resulting document embeddings to
predict the MeSH label vector.

\subsection{PubMedBERT}

\subsubsection{Input Representation}

We concatenate title and abstract using the \texttt{[SEP]} token:
\[
    x = \texttt{[CLS]}\ t\ \texttt{[SEP]}\ a\ \texttt{[SEP]}
\]
The resulting sequence is truncated to 512 tokens, the maximum context length of BERT-based models.

\subsubsection{Model Architecture}

We fine-tune PubMedBERT (\texttt{microsoft/BiomedNLP-BiomedBERT-base-uncased-abstract-fulltext})
as our encoder. The \texttt{[CLS]} token representation from the final layer is passed through a
dropout layer ($p = 0.1$) and a linear classification head:
\[
    \hat{y} = \sigma\!\left(W \cdot \text{Dropout}(h_{\texttt{CLS}})\right)
\]
where $W \in \mathbf{R}^{|L| \times d}$, $d = 768$ is the hidden size, and $\sigma$ is the
sigmoid function applied element-wise.

\subsubsection{Loss Function}

We use the Asymmetric Loss (ASL)~\cite{ridnik2021asl}, which separately controls the contribution
of positive and negative examples via asymmetric focusing parameters. ASL is particularly effective
for multi-label problems with many negative labels per sample, as is the case here.

\subsubsection{Training Details}

We use the AdamW optimiser with a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$, a cosine schedule with
10\% warmup, gradient clipping at 1.0, and batch size 16. Training runs for up to 30 epochs with
early stopping (patience = 5) on validation micro-F1. Mixed precision (bfloat16) is used on CUDA.
The prediction threshold $\tau$ is tuned on the validation set after training.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a comparison of a Word2Vec + MLP baseline against a fine-tuned PubMedBERT model
for multi-label MeSH term classification on a coverage-sampled subset of the BioASQ 2022 dataset.
The coverage-first sampling strategy ensures all 29{,}681 MeSH labels are represented in the
training data despite using only a fraction of the full corpus.
PubMedBERT substantially outperforms the baseline, demonstrating the value of domain-specific
pre-training for biomedical text classification.
