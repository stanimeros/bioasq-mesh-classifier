\section{Introduction}

Medical literature is indexed in PubMed using MeSH (Medical Subject Headings), a controlled vocabulary
maintained by the National Library of Medicine. Manually assigning MeSH terms to the growing volume of
biomedical publications is time-consuming and expensive. Automatic biomedical indexing, the task of
predicting MeSH terms from an article's text, therefore has significant practical value.

This paper presents our approach to the BioASQ Task A challenge~\cite{bioasq2015}, which frames the
problem as multi-label classification: each article may be assigned multiple MeSH terms simultaneously.
We focus exclusively on the title and abstract as input, and leverage pre-trained biomedical language
models, specifically BioBERT~\cite{lee2020biobert}, to learn contextual representations.

The rest of this paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.
Section~\ref{sec:method} describes our approach. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents experimental
results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Early approaches to biomedical article indexing relied on TF-IDF weighted bag-of-words representations
combined with k-nearest neighbours (MTI,~\cite{mti}) or SVMs. The introduction of word embeddings,
such as Word2Vec~\cite{mikolov2013} and fastText~\cite{joulin2017}, improved performance by capturing
semantic similarity between medical terms.

More recently, transformer-based models pre-trained on large biomedical corpora have shown strong results.
BioBERT~\cite{lee2020biobert} and SciBERT~\cite{beltagy2019scibert} both extend BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert}
with domain-specific pre-training and have achieved state-of-the-art performance on multiple biomedical
NLP benchmarks. In the BioASQ challenge, fine-tuned transformer models consistently outperform shallow
approaches, with MeSH-CNN~\cite{meshcnn} and AttentionMeSH~\cite{attentionmesh} being notable prior
systems.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given the title $t$ and abstract $a$ of a biomedical article, the goal is to predict a subset
$\hat{Y} \subseteq L$ of MeSH labels from the full label set $L$.
This is a multi-label classification problem with $|L|$ binary outputs.

\subsection{Input Representation}

We concatenate title and abstract using the \texttt{[SEP]} token:
\[
    x = \texttt{[CLS]}\ t\ \texttt{[SEP]}\ a\ \texttt{[SEP]}
\]
The resulting sequence is truncated to 512 tokens, the maximum context length of BERT-based models.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

We fine-tune BioBERT (\texttt{dmis-lab/biobert-base-cased-v1.2}) as our encoder. The \texttt{[CLS]}
token representation from the final layer is passed through a dropout layer and a linear classification
head:
\[
    \hat{y} = \sigma\!\left(W \cdot \text{Dropout}(h_{\texttt{CLS}})\right)
\]
where $W \in \mathbf{R}^{|L| \times d}$, $d = 768$ is the hidden size, and $\sigma$ is the
sigmoid function applied element-wise. We use binary cross-entropy loss:
\[
    \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{N|L|} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{|L|}
    \left[ y_{ij} \log \hat{y}_{ij} + (1 - y_{ij}) \log (1 - \hat{y}_{ij}) \right]
\]

\subsection{Training Details}

We use the AdamW optimiser with a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$ and gradient clipping at $1.0$.
MeSH labels appearing fewer than 10 times in the training set are discarded to reduce the output
vocabulary and mitigate extreme class imbalance. Labels are predicted at inference time by applying a
threshold $\tau = 0.5$ to the sigmoid outputs.
